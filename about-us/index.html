Hi, I’m Daniel Parker. I’m originally from Melbourne, Australia, but for the past five years, I’ve been living in Nanjing, China, working in the field of import and export trade. My work connects me with people and markets all over the world, but my heart is firmly anchored here in this city of history, culture, and quiet surprises.


Nanjing is a place where the past and present walk side by side. On one hand, you have the towering modern skyline, international trade hubs, and busy streets full of energy. On the other, you have centuries-old city walls, tranquil gardens, and echoes of stories that go back more than a thousand years. As someone whose job is about movement—products crossing oceans, cultures meeting at borders—I find it grounding to live in a city that has witnessed so many crossroads of history.


One of the things I love most about living here is how easy it is to slip from the urban rush into serene beauty. On weekday mornings, I might be in the office negotiating shipments and reviewing contracts. But on weekends, I’m often exploring Nanjing’s surrounding landscapes—cycling around Xuanwu Lake, hiking the trails of Purple Mountain, or wandering the quiet courtyards of Ming Xiaoling Mausoleum.


My travel style is deeply shaped by living here. I’m not just looking for “tourist spots”; I’m searching for what I call “layers”—the small details that reveal a place’s personality. In Nanjing, that could mean a hidden alley lined with old teahouses, a view of the Yangtze River at sunset, or a street vendor who remembers your order from last time.


Being based in Nanjing also makes short trips incredibly rewarding. Within a couple of hours, I can be in ancient water towns like Wuzhen or Tongli, where wooden bridges arch over narrow canals and life moves at a slower pace. Sometimes I take the high-speed train to Hangzhou for a weekend of West Lake strolls and long chats over Dragon Well tea. Other times, I drive out to the countryside in Anhui Province to hike among terraced fields and white-walled villages.


As someone in the import/export business, I often think about how trade routes are like travel routes—they both carry stories. The same way goods pass from one country to another, cultures leave traces in each other. When I travel, I’m looking for those traces. For example, visiting Suzhou and seeing how its gardens blend philosophy, art, and daily life gave me insight into how Chinese aesthetics influence design in unexpected ways.


Living in Nanjing has also made me more conscious of seasonal rhythms. Spring here is a painter’s dream—plum blossoms in full bloom at Meihua Mountain, cherry blossoms lining the paths in Jiming Temple. Summer evenings bring night markets alive, with the air full of grilled skewer smoke and laughter. Autumn turns the city golden, especially in the ginkgo-lined streets near the Presidential Palace. Winter is quieter, but there’s something deeply peaceful about standing by the Qinhuai River with a cup of hot tea as snow begins to fall.


I’ve also learned that travel doesn’t have to mean “far.” Sometimes the most memorable trips happen within an hour’s reach. One of my favorite escapes is to take a slow ferry across the Yangtze just to watch the city skyline change as the boat drifts. Another is spending a day in nearby Jurong, exploring bamboo forests and tiny local eateries that don’t even have names on the door.


Working in trade teaches you to value connection, and travel, for me, is about connecting—with landscapes, with people, and with the stories that make each place unique. I often return from a trip not just with photos, but with flavors, scents, and conversations that stay with me. I might bring back handmade ceramics from Yixing, or tea leaves from a farmer I met in Zhejiang, and share them with friends over dinner.


Looking ahead, I hope to keep using Nanjing as my base for exploring both China and the world. The city’s location, its mix of tradition and modernity, and its easy access to so many fascinating places make it the perfect hub for a traveler’s life. My bucket list includes more off-the-beaten-path destinations in Jiangsu Province, and perhaps a long road trip tracing the Yangtze River upstream.


For now, I’m happy here—balancing the fast pace of international trade with the slow rewards of travel. If you ever pass through Nanjing, you might find me at a lakeside café with my camera, a notebook, and a train ticket to somewhere I’ve never been before. After all, life’s best journeys often start with a simple decision to go.


— Daniel Parker
