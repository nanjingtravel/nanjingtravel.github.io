Exploring Nanjing’s Food Scene: Hidden Gems

  • Home
  • Travel Blog
  • Blog Details

Nanjing, the ancient capital of China, is a city steeped in history, culture, and—most importantly—mouthwatering cuisine. While travelers often flock to its famous landmarks like the Confucius Temple or Sun Yat-sen Mausoleum, the city’s food scene remains an underrated treasure trove. From hole-in-the-wall eateries to bustling night markets, Nanjing offers a culinary adventure that’s as rich as its heritage. Let’s dive into some of the city’s best-kept secrets.

The Heart of Nanjing’s Street Food

Laomendong: A Street Food Paradise

Tucked away near the Zhonghua Gate, Laomendong is a labyrinth of narrow alleys where the aroma of sizzling skewers and steaming pots fills the air. This area is a haven for street food lovers, offering everything from crispy jianbing (savory crepes) to choudoufu (stinky tofu)—a divisive but iconic snack.

One standout is Huangji Rolled Noodles, a tiny stall known for its roujiamo (Chinese hamburger). The tender braised pork, stuffed inside a flaky, freshly baked bun, is a must-try. For dessert, don’t miss the tanghulu (candied hawthorns) sold by vendors along the main strip—a sweet-and-sour treat that’s perfect for balancing out the savory flavors.

Shiziqiao Night Market: Late-Night Bites

If you’re a night owl, Shiziqiao Night Market is your go-to spot. Open until the early hours, this market buzzes with locals grabbing post-midnight snacks. The star here is duck blood vermicelli soup (鸭血粉丝汤, yaxue fansi tang), a Nanjing specialty. The rich broth, infused with duck blood, tofu, and vermicelli, is surprisingly comforting.

Another crowd-pleaser is grilled squid, slathered in a spicy-sweet sauce and cooked over open flames. Pair it with a cold Nanjing beer for the full experience.

Hidden Restaurants Worth the Hunt

Nanjing Impressions (南京大牌档)

While not exactly “hidden,” Nanjing Impressions is often overlooked by tourists in favor of more famous chains. This restaurant is a love letter to Jiangsu cuisine, with an ambiance that mimics old-world teahouses. Order their saltwater duck (盐水鸭, yanshui ya), a local delicacy that’s tender, fragrant, and perfectly seasoned.

For something unique, try lion’s head meatballs (狮子头, shizitou)—succulent pork balls simmered in a savory broth. The theatrical presentation, complete with staff in traditional attire, adds to the charm.

A Tiny Dumpling Shop You’ve Never Heard Of

Deep in the Qinhuai District, there’s an unassuming dumpling joint called Xiaolongbao King. Their soup dumplings (小笼包, xiaolongbao) are legendary among locals—thin-skinned, bursting with hot broth, and filled with juicy pork. The trick? Dip them in black vinegar and ginger to cut through the richness.

Sweet Treats and Local Delicacies

Qinhuai Snacks: More Than Just Desserts

The Qinhuai River area isn’t just for sightseeing—it’s also home to some of Nanjing’s best sweets. Osmanthus cake (桂花糕, guihua gao) is a fragrant, slightly sticky dessert made with rice flour and sweet osmanthus flowers. You’ll find vendors selling them near the riverbanks, especially in autumn when the flowers bloom.

Another must-try is sweet fermented rice balls (酒酿圆子, jiuniang yuanzi), a warm, comforting dessert with a hint of alcohol from fermented glutinous rice. It’s the perfect way to end a chilly evening.

The Secret Tea House

For a break from the hustle, head to Chá Yuán (茶缘), a hidden tea house near Xuanwu Lake. Their biluochun tea, a local green tea, is served with traditional snacks like sesame candies and mung bean cakes. The serene atmosphere makes it a great spot to recharge.

Pro Tips for Food Explorers

  • Timing is everything: Many street vendors only operate in the early morning or late evening. Plan accordingly!
  • Follow the locals: If a place has a line, it’s probably worth the wait.
  • Don’t shy away from “weird” foods: Nanjing’s cuisine includes unique dishes like duck blood soup—embrace the adventure.

Nanjing’s food scene is a journey through flavors, history, and local life. Whether you’re slurping noodles at a midnight market or savoring tea by the lake, every bite tells a story. So ditch the tourist traps and let your taste buds lead the way.
