Interview with a Nanjing Playwright

  • Home
  • Travel Blog
  • Blog Details

The golden ginkgo leaves were falling like delicate coins onto the ancient city wall. I wasn’t in Nanjing just for the autumn spectacle, though it was a breathtaking bonus. I was here to meet a man who doesn’t just walk these historic streets but who listens to their whispers and translates them for the stage. His name is Wei, a playwright whose work is deeply entwined with the soul of this former capital. We met in a quiet teahouse tucked away in the Laomendong district, the air thick with the scent of biluochun and history. What followed was less an interview and more a journey into the very essence of what makes Nanjing a timeless destination.

More Than a Backdrop: The City as a Character

I started by asking Wei the most obvious question: how does a city like Nanjing, with its immense weight of history, influence a storyteller?

He smiled, sipping his tea thoughtfully. "Most cities are settings," he began. "Paris is romantic, New York is fast, Tokyo is neon-futuristic. They are beautiful stages for a play. But Nanjing... Nanjing is not the stage. Nanjing is the lead actor. It has a personality, a mood, a tragic past, and a resilient spirit. You cannot write a story set in Nanjing; you must write a story with Nanjing."

The Whisper of Purple Mountain

"Take Zijinshan (Purple Mountain)," he said, gesturing vaguely in the direction of the famed hill. "Tourists go for the Sun Yat-sen Mausoleum, the Ming Xiaoling Mausoleum. They climb the steps, take photos, and check it off their list. But for me, the mountain is a keeper of secrets. Every emperor buried there, every soldier who fell, every leaf that has grown and died—they all add a layer to the soil. When I write a scene of reflection or grief, I imagine my characters feeling the gaze of that mountain. It’s not just a scenic spot; it’s a silent, ancient witness. That’s the feeling I try to capture—the profound sense of time that hangs over this city, a feeling every traveler subtly absorbs, even if they can't name it."

The Qinhuai River: A Flow of Memory and Modernity

Our conversation drifted to the Qinhuai River, the lifeblood of old Nanjing, now a major tourist hub with its lantern-lit boats and bustling snack streets.

"The Qinhuai is a perfect metaphor for Nanjing itself," Wei explained. "On the surface, you have the colorful tourist boats, the bright lights, the vendors selling tangdui and other sweets. It's vibrant, alive, a fantastic spectacle. This is the 'travel hotspot' everyone posts on social media. But beneath that surface flow deeper, darker currents—stories of the Jinling era, scholars and courtesans from the Ming and Qing dynasties, tales of love and betrayal. My play 'Lanterns on the Quiet River' is about that duality. It’s about a modern tour guide who starts hearing echoes of the past. The tourism experience is the gateway; the stories are the real destination."

He leaned forward, his eyes alight with passion. "This is what smart travel should be. Don't just ride the boat. Sit on the bank afterward. Imagine the scholars who once wrote poetry here. Feel the layers. That’s when you stop being a tourist and start being a traveler."

Weaving History into the Fabric of Modern Tourism

I was curious about how his work interacts with the city's deliberate efforts to market its history. Is there a tension between artistic truth and tourist-friendly narratives?

"It's a dance," he admitted. "The city promotes the 'Ancient Capital' brand, and that’s valid. But history is not always clean and heroic. Nanjing has known immense tragedy, most profoundly in 1937. A playwright, and indeed a thoughtful visitor, cannot ignore this. However, you don't build a tourism industry solely on sorrow. The challenge and the beauty lie in presenting a holistic portrait."

Beyond the Memorial Hall: Finding Resilience

"Many visitors come to the Nanjing Massacre Memorial Hall. It is a necessary, harrowing pilgrimage. But they often leave burdened by a singular grief. My work tries to answer the question that naturally follows: 'And then what?' The 'then what' is the story of Nanjing's people. It's the story of rebuilding, of a spirit that refuses to be extinguished. This resilience is present in the vibrant art districts like the one at the Jiangning Fabric Market, where old industrial spaces are reborn. It's in the determined cheer of the Fuzimiao (Confucius Temple) area. I try to channel this resilience into my characters. It tells the visitor that while it's crucial to remember the pain, it's also vital to witness the incredible strength that emerged from it. This completes the historical journey."

The Humble Xiaolongbao as a Time Capsule

Wei surprised me by pivoting to food. "You cannot talk about tourism here without talking about food. And for me, the humble xiaolongbao is a perfect piece of theatre. Think about it. You go to a famous restaurant, perhaps one near the Confucius Temple. There's a long queue—a tourist hotspot in itself. You get your basket. The exterior is delicate, almost transparent. That's the beautiful, fragile facade of history. Then you take a bite, and the hot, flavorful soup bursts out. That's the emotional, often complex, and surprising core of the city's story. It’s an immediate, sensory experience that connects you to generations of Nanjing residents. Food is the most accessible form of cultural immersion."

The Playwright's Personal Travel Tips: Seeing the Unseen

As our tea grew cold, I asked him for his personal, off-the-beaten-path recommendations for travelers who want to see the Nanjing he knows.

"First," he said, "wake up early. See the city before the tour buses arrive. Walk the Ming City Wall near the Jimen section when the morning mist is still rising from the Xuanwu Lake. You'll have it mostly to yourself, and you can feel the stones breathing."

"Second, get lost in the alleyways, the hutongs, behind the main streets. The real life of Nanjing happens in these narrow lanes—the sound of mahjong tiles, the scent of laundry, the old men playing chess. This is the rhythm of the city."

"And finally," he concluded, "talk to people. Not just guides. Talk to the shopkeeper selling yunjin brocade, a craft that is itself a piece of living history. Talk to the student sketching in the park. Their stories are the contemporary scripts being written every day. My plays are just an extension of those everyday conversations."

The evening faded into night, and the lanterns in Laomendong began to glow, casting a warm, golden light. Leaving the teahouse, I didn't just see a beautiful, historic city. I saw a living, breathing character, just as Wei had described—proud, complex, and endlessly fascinating. The tree-lined boulevards were no longer just pretty streets; they were the arteries of a storyteller's inspiration. The hum of the city was no longer just noise; it was the soundtrack to a thousand untold plays, waiting for a curious traveler to stop and listen.
