Nanjing DIY Travel: Save Money and Have Fun

  • Home
  • Travel Blog
  • Blog Details

Nanjing, the capital of China’s Jiangsu province, is a city where ancient history and modern life blend seamlessly. Often overshadowed by its flashy neighbor Shanghai, Nanjing offers a rich, layered travel experience without the hefty price tag. DIY travel here isn’t just about saving money—it’s about crafting a personal journey through imperial palaces, poignant memorials, vibrant food streets, and serene natural landscapes. This guide will show you how to dive deep into Nanjing’s soul, enjoy its top attractions, and discover hidden gems, all while keeping your budget firmly in check.

Why Choose Nanjing for Your DIY Adventure?

Nanjing is a dream for independent travelers. The city’s comprehensive and affordable public transportation system, including a user-friendly metro, makes navigating a breeze. Unlike more tourist-saturated cities, Nanjing allows for spontaneity. You can wander through a 600-year-old city wall gate in the morning and sip artisan coffee in a trendy district in the afternoon, all on your own schedule. The cost of living is significantly lower than in Beijing or Shanghai, meaning your money goes much further. From street food feasts to budget-friendly accommodations, Nanjing empowers you to have an authentic and immersive experience without the guided tour markup.

The Allure of the Former Capital

As the former capital of several Chinese dynasties, most notably the Ming Dynasty, Nanjing is steeped in a regal history that is palpable as you walk its tree-lined boulevards. The city’s name, meaning "Southern Capital," hints at its historical significance. This isn't just a place to see history in a museum; it's a place where you can touch the very stones that emperors walked upon. The vibe is more contemplative and scholarly than commercial, offering a refreshing break from the relentless pace of China’s megacities.

Crafting Your Budget-Friendly Nanjing Itinerary

A well-planned itinerary is the backbone of any successful DIY trip. Here’s a sample three-day plan designed to maximize experience while minimizing cost.

Day 1: Imperial Grandeur and City Walls

Begin your journey at the heart of ancient Nanjing: the Ming Xiaoling Mausoleum. This UNESCO World Heritage site is the tomb of the Hongwu Emperor, the founder of the Ming Dynasty. The Spirit Way, a path lined with massive stone statues of animals and mythical guardians, is a breathtaking prelude to the main tomb. The sheer scale and peaceful atmosphere of the surrounding Zhongshan Mountain Scenic Area are unforgettable. To save money, purchase a combined ticket that often includes other nearby attractions.

In the afternoon, head to the City Wall of Nanjing. Specifically, the Jiming Temple section offers a fantastic walk with stunning views of the temple, the Xuanwu Lake, and the modern city skyline. Walking or biking on this ancient fortification is a quintessential Nanjing experience. Rent a bike for a small fee and enjoy a leisurely ride along one of the best-preserved ancient city walls in the world.

As evening falls, make your way to the Confucius Temple (Fuzimiao) Qinhuai Scenic Area. While the temple itself is interesting, the real magic is in the surrounding area. Stroll along the illuminated Qinhuai River, watch traditional performances, and browse the bustling market streets. This is a touristy spot, but the energy and beautiful lights are well worth it. For dinner, skip the overpriced riverside restaurants and dive into the side alleys for more authentic and affordable local snacks.

Day 2: Reflection, History, and Natural Beauty

Prepare for a somber but essential day. Visit the Memorial Hall of the Victims in the Nanjing Massacre by Japanese Invaders. This is a profoundly moving and important site that documents a tragic chapter in world history. Admission is free, but it requires emotional fortitude. It’s a powerful reminder of the resilience of the human spirit. Plan to spend a few quiet hours here.

Afterward, seek solace in nature at Xuanwu Lake Park. This massive park, located right in the city center, is a favorite among locals for walking, boating, and simply relaxing. Entrance to the park is free, though you may pay a small fee for activities like renting a paddle boat on the lake. It's the perfect place to decompress and reflect.

For your second evening, explore the 1912 Fashion District. This area, named after the year the Republic of China was founded, is a hub of nightlife set in renovated historical buildings. While some bars and clubs can be pricey, you can easily enjoy the atmosphere by taking a stroll and perhaps grabbing a drink at a more casual, low-key establishment. It’s a great spot for people-watching and seeing Nanjing’s modern, youthful side.

Day 3: Presidential Politics and Culinary Delights

Start your day at the Sun Yat-sen Mausoleum. Perched on the southern slope of Purple Mountain (Zijinshan), this magnificent mausoleum honors the founding father of modern China, Dr. Sun Yat-sen. The climb up the 392 steps is rewarded with a magnificent view and a chance to see the solemn statue of the great leader. Again, look for combination tickets for the Purple Mountain area to save on entrance fees.

Next, visit the nearby Former Presidential Palace. This massive complex has served as the headquarters for various Chinese governments throughout history, from the Taiping Heavenly Kingdom to the Republic of China. Wandering through its gardens and historic offices feels like walking through a living textbook. The audio guide is a worthwhile investment for a deeper understanding.

Your final culinary adventure should be on Shiziqiao (Lion Bridge) Food Street or exploring the backstreets around the Confucius Temple. This is where you’ll find the real foodie gems. Be brave and try local specialties like Salt-Baked Duck (a Nanjing classic), Tangbao (soup dumplings), and various pancake and noodle dishes. Pointing at what looks good is a perfectly valid ordering strategy and part of the fun.

Mastering the Art of Saving Money in Nanjing

Getting Around on a Dime

Nanjing’s metro system is your best friend. It’s clean, efficient, and signs are in both Chinese and English. Purchase a rechargeable IC card (like the Jinlingtong) for discounted fares and the convenience of not buying single-journey tickets for every trip. Public buses are even cheaper and reach areas the metro doesn't, though they can be challenging for non-Mandarin speakers. For short distances, Didi (China's Uber) is affordable, especially if you're splitting the fare.

Eating Like a Local for Less

Forget fancy restaurants. The true flavor of Nanjing is on the street and in local canteens. * Breakfast: Start your day at a local breakfast stall. A jianbing (savory crepe) or a serving of doujiang (soy milk) and youtiao (fried dough stick) costs just a few dollars. * Lunch & Dinner: Seek out food courts in shopping malls or small, family-run restaurants where locals are eating. A hearty bowl of noodles or a plate of yangchun noodles is incredibly satisfying and cheap. The key is to look for places with pictures on the menu or plastic food models, making ordering easy.

Affordable Accommodation Choices

You don’t need a five-star hotel to have a comfortable stay. * Hostels: Nanjing has excellent hostels, particularly in the Confucius Temple area. They offer cheap dorm beds or private rooms, free Wi-Fi, and a great opportunity to meet other travelers. Many also organize budget-friendly tours or social events. * Budget Hotels: Chinese chains like Hanting, Jinjiang Inn, and 7 Days Inn provide clean, no-frills rooms in convenient locations for a very reasonable price. Book through apps like Trip.com or Ctrip for the best deals.

Smart Sightseeing Strategies

  • Student IDs: If you are a student, always carry your international student ID card. Many attractions in China offer significant discounts for students.
  • Combo Tickets: As mentioned, always ask about combination tickets for scenic areas like Purple Mountain. They can save you up to 50% compared to buying individual tickets.
  • Free Attractions: Don’t underestimate the power of free activities. Walking along the Qinhuai River, exploring the Xuanwu Lake Park, wandering through the Nanjing University campus, or hiking in the Purple Mountain foothills are all completely free and wonderfully rewarding experiences.

Beyond the Guidebook: Unique DIY Experiences

To truly make your trip unforgettable, step off the beaten path.

Explore a Creative Hub: The Yihe Road (Yhe Lù)

This area is Nanjing's equivalent of Beijing's 798 Art District, but quieter and more integrated into a historical neighborhood. Here, you'll find repurposed民国 (Mínguó - Republic of China era) villas that now house art galleries, independent coffee shops, boutique stores, and quirky cafes. It’s a perfect place to spend a lazy afternoon, soaking in the artistic vibe and enjoying a cup of locally roasted coffee.

A Walk Through Literature at Nanjing Library

For a quiet escape, visit one of China's largest libraries. Even if you don't read Chinese, the architecture and the sight of locals immersed in study is a fascinating cultural insight. The atmosphere is one of calm and concentration, a world away from the bustling city outside.

Hike the Zijinshan Observatory Trail

For the active traveler, skip the cable car and hike up Purple Mountain to the Zijinshan Astronomical Observatory. The trail takes you through beautiful, dense forests, and the observatory itself, one of the most important in China, offers a unique perspective and panoramic views of the city. It’s a less crowded and more active way to experience the mountain.

Nanjing is a city that rewards the curious and the independent. With a little planning and a spirit of adventure, you can uncover its majestic history, engage with its vibrant present, and create memories that will last a lifetime, all without straining your wallet. So pack your bags, grab a map, and get ready to write your own story in this captivating former capital.
