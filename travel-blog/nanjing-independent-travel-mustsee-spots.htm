Nanjing Independent Travel: Must-See Spots

  • Home
  • Travel Blog
  • Blog Details

Nanjing, the capital of China’s Jiangsu province, is a city where the weight of history and the pulse of modern life coexist in a fascinating, sometimes poignant, harmony. Often overshadowed by its flashier neighbors like Shanghai and Hangzhou, Nanjing is a destination for the thoughtful traveler, one who seeks to understand the soul of China beyond the skyscrapers. It’s a city of profound beauty, from the crimson leaves of Qixia Mountain to the serene waters of Xuanwu Lake, all set against a backdrop of monumental history. This guide is your key to unlocking the must-see spots of this captivating city, ensuring your independent travel experience is as rich and rewarding as the city itself.

The Historical Heart: Echoes of Ancient Capitals

Nanjing’s name, meaning "Southern Capital," is a testament to its role as the seat of power for several Chinese dynasties. To walk its streets is to walk through layers of time, and no visit is complete without immersing yourself in its historical core.

Dr. Sun Yat-sen's Mausoleum

Perched majestically on the southern slopes of Purple Mountain (Zijin Shan), the Mausoleum of Dr. Sun Yat-sen is not just a tomb; it's a national monument. Dr. Sun Yat-sen is revered as the "Father of Modern China," and his final resting place reflects his immense importance. The journey begins at a grand paifang (memorial archway), leading you up a long, stepped path flanked by lush pines and cypresses. The climb, consisting of 392 steps, is symbolic and offers a progressively breathtaking view. The sense of anticipation builds with each flight until you reach the main hall, a magnificent blue-roofed structure inscribed with his principles. Inside, a statue of Dr. Sun sits serenely, and the atmosphere is one of deep respect and tranquility. The view from the top, looking back over the staircase and the sprawling city beyond, is worth every step. Go early in the morning to avoid the crowds and experience the site in the peaceful morning light.

The Ming Xiaoling Mausoleum

A short walk or shuttle ride from Sun Yat-sen's Mausoleum lies another monumental tomb, that of the Hongwu Emperor, the founder of the Ming Dynasty. The Ming Xiaoling Mausoleum is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and represents the peak of imperial tomb architecture. Unlike Sun Yat-sen's mausoleum, the approach here is along the Sacred Way (Shendao), a spirit path guarded by towering, weathered stone statues of lions, camels, elephants, and mythical beasts. These silent sentinels have stood for centuries, creating an awe-inspiring and slightly mystical atmosphere. The path meanders, supposedly to confuse evil spirits, leading you through a beautiful, forested landscape. The tomb itself is a large, grassy tumulus, as the underground palace has never been excavated. The scale and artistry of the entire complex offer a powerful glimpse into the world of China's imperial past.

The City Wall of Nanjing

To truly grasp the scale of old Nanjing, you must walk a section of its magnificent city wall. Built in the 14th century by the Hongwu Emperor, it was once the longest city wall in the world. While much of it is gone, significant, beautifully restored sections remain. The most popular and scenic stretch runs along the banks of the Qinhuai River and Xuanwu Lake. Renting a bike or simply taking a leisurely stroll along the top of the wall provides a unique perspective of the city. You'll see the contrast between the ancient, moss-covered stones and the modern skyline, with locals flying kites and practicing tai chi in the shadows below. The Zhonghua Gate (Zhonghuamen) is a particular highlight—a massive, fortress-like gate complex with multiple layers and hidden chambers, illustrating the sophisticated defensive strategies of the time.

Reflection and Remembrance: Nanjing's 20th Century

Nanjing’s history is not without profound tragedy. A visit to the city requires acknowledging the darker chapters, which are memorialized with dignity and a plea for peace.

The Nanjing Massacre Memorial Hall

This is a difficult but essential visit. The Memorial Hall of the Victims of the Nanjing Massacre by the Japanese Invaders commemorates the horrific events that took place in the city in 1937. The museum is a masterclass in somber, powerful design. The exterior is dominated by stark, grey walls and sculptures depicting the suffering of the victims. Inside, the exhibits are meticulously curated, presenting historical evidence, personal accounts, and artifacts that tell the story with unflinching honesty. The "Hall of Bones" is particularly moving, containing the remains of victims exhumed from one of the many massacre sites. The mood is heavy, and silence is the only appropriate response. The final message, however, is not one of hatred, but of peace and the importance of remembering history to prevent its repetition. It is a deeply affecting experience that will stay with you long after you leave.

Presidential Palace

A single complex that tells the story of China's turbulent transition from empire to republic, the Presidential Palace is a sprawling site that served as the center of power for the Taiping Heavenly Kingdom, the Qing government, and finally, the Republic of China under Chiang Kai-shek. Wandering through its halls and gardens is like flipping through the pages of a history book. You can see the opulent meeting rooms of the Taiping leadership, the Western-influenced offices of Sun Yat-sen, and the war command center used during the Sino-Japanese War. The beautifully landscaped gardens offer a moment of peace and reflection. The palace doesn't glorify any single era but presents itself as a neutral witness to the relentless flow of history, making it a profoundly thought-provoking stop.

The Soul of the City: Culture, Cuisine, and Canals

Beyond the grand monuments and solemn memorials, the real Nanjing lives in its bustling streets, along its ancient canals, and at its dining tables.

Confucius Temple (Fuzimiao) and Qinhuai River

This area is the historic cultural and entertainment hub of Nanjing. Centered around a temple dedicated to the great philosopher Confucius, the Fuzimiao district is now a vibrant, sometimes touristy, area of restored Ming and Qing-style architecture, shops, and street food stalls. The real magic happens along the Qinhuai River, which winds its way through the neighborhood. As dusk falls, the traditional buildings and arched bridges are illuminated by red lanterns, casting a beautiful reflection on the water. A boat ride on the Qinhuai River is a must-do; it’s a serene way to take in the scenery and imagine the scholars and courtesans who once frequented these waterways. Don't miss the opportunity to try local snacks here—it's a food lover's paradise.

Nanjing's Culinary Scene: A Feast for the Senses

Nanjing cuisine, part of the Jiangsu culinary tradition, is known for its emphasis on freshness and subtle, balanced flavors. A food tour is a critical part of any visit. * Nanjing Salted Duck (Yanshui Ya): This is the city's most famous dish. Unlike the roasted Peking duck, Nanjing's version is brined and stewed, resulting in tender, flavorful meat with a smooth, non-greasy skin. It's a ubiquitous sight in markets and restaurants. * Duck Blood and Vermicelli Soup (Yaxue Fensi Tang): A beloved local comfort food. This savory soup features chunks of congealed duck blood, vermicelli noodles, and various offal in a rich, peppery broth. It's far more delicious than it might sound to the uninitiated. * Soup Dumplings (Tangbao): While Shanghai is famous for its xiaolongbao, Nanjing has its own fantastic version. These are often larger, with a thin skin and a rich, hot broth inside. * Street Food at Lao Men Dong: For the adventurous eater, a visit to the Lao Men Dong (Old Gate East) area is essential. This historic lane is packed with vendors selling everything from candied hawthorn berries (bingtanghulu) to stinky tofu and pan-fried buns.

Natural Escapes: The Greener Side of Nanjing

For a city with such a dense history, Nanjing is remarkably green, offering plenty of opportunities to escape the urban hustle.

Xuanwu Lake

Located right in the heart of the city, just outside the city wall, Xuanwu Lake is an enormous and beautiful urban park. Its five islets are connected by causeways and bridges, perfect for a long, leisurely stroll or a bike ride. You can rent a paddleboat to get out on the water, visit the small temples and pagodas on the islands, or simply find a bench and watch the locals go about their daily exercises. The skyline of modern Nanjing provides a stunning backdrop, especially at sunset. It's the city's lungs and a favorite weekend retreat for residents.

Qixia Mountain (Qixia Shan)

A short trip northeast of the city center, Qixia Mountain is a renowned scenic area, famous for its ancient Buddhist temple and, most of all, its stunning autumn foliage. The Qixia Temple, dating back to the 5th century, is a serene complex nestled in the hills. But the main event is the maple trees. In late autumn, the entire mountainside erupts in a blaze of red, orange, and yellow, attracting photographers and nature lovers from all over. Hiking the trails through this colorful landscape to the thousand Buddha Cliff, with its hundreds of carved niches and statues, is a truly magical experience.

Practical Tips for the Independent Traveler

  • Getting Around: Nanjing's metro system is clean, efficient, inexpensive, and well-signposted in English. It connects almost all the major attractions. Didi (China's ride-hailing app) and taxis are also readily available.
  • Best Time to Visit: Autumn (September to November) is ideal. The weather is mild, and the fall colors at Qixia Mountain are spectacular. Spring (March to May) is also lovely, though it can be rainier. Summers are hot and humid, while winters are cold and damp.
  • Accommodation: For first-time visitors, staying near the Xinjiekou area puts you at the city's transportation and commercial hub, making it easy to get anywhere. For a more historic vibe, consider a hotel near the Confucius Temple area.
  • Language: While English is not widely spoken outside major hotels and tourist attractions, people are generally helpful. Having your destination written down in Chinese characters on your phone and using translation apps will be invaluable.
