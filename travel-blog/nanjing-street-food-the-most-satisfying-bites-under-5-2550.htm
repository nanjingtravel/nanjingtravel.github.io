Nanjing Street Food: The Most Satisfying Bites Under $5

  • Home
  • Travel Blog
  • Blog Details

Nanjing, a city where ancient history collides with modern vibrancy, offers one of China’s most thrilling culinary adventures. Forget fancy restaurants—the real magic happens on the streets, where sizzling woks, smoky grills, and steaming baskets serve up flavors that punch way above their price tag. For travelers craving authenticity without breaking the bank, here’s your ultimate guide to Nanjing’s unmissable street eats—all under $5.

The Must-Try Classics

1. Duck Blood Vermicelli Soup (Yā Xuè Fěn Sī Tāng)

A Nanjing icon, this bowl of comfort combines silky vermicelli, tender duck blood cubes, and a rich, peppery broth. Vendors near Confucius Temple (Fūzǐ Miào) ladle it out for about $2.50—add a sprinkle of cilantro and chili oil for extra kick. Pro tip: Pair it with a shaobing (crispy sesame flatbread) for the ultimate texture contrast.

2. Pan-Fried Beef Buns (Niúròu Guōtiē)

These golden, crispy-bottomed buns ooze juicy beef and scallion filling. At just $1 per piece, they’re a steal. Head to Laomendong (Old Gate East), where street stalls flip them fresh off cast-iron pans. The crunch-to-fluff ratio? Perfection.

3. Salted Duck (Yánshuǐ Yā)

Nanjing’s answer to Peking duck—but cheaper and packed with umami. Marinated in brine with star anise, the meat is fall-off-the-bone tender. A quarter-duck portion costs around $4 at Zhāng Hóngxīng, a century-old shop near Xinjiekou. Eat it wrapped in pancakes or straight off the bone.

Hidden Gems Locals Love

1. Sticky Rice Stuffed Lotus Root (Guìhuā Táng Ǒu)

Sweet, sticky, and slightly floral, this snack is a textural dream: glutinous rice packed into lotus root holes, slow-cooked, then drizzled with osmanthus syrup. Find it at Qinhuai Snack Street for under $3.

2. Grilled Squid Skewers (Tiěbǎn Yóuyú)

For seafood lovers, the smoky aroma of squid sizzling on hotplates is irresistible. Brushed with chili-soy glaze and served on bamboo sticks ($1.50 each), these are a late-night staple near Hunan Road’s night market.

3. Sweet Wine Soup (Jiǔniàng Tāngyuán)

A warm, boozy dessert soup with fermented rice and chewy tangyuan (sweet dumplings). It’s like liquid gold on chilly evenings, and a bowl costs just $2 at Gǔlóu Dumpling House.

Where to Feast Like a Local

Confucius Temple Area

The epicenter of street food, especially along Pingjiang Road. Go early—vendors sell out fast!

Shiziqiao Night Market

Open after sunset, this rowdy alley is where students and locals devour skewers, jianbing (savory crepes), and more. Budget $5 to leave stuffed.

Laomendong Historic District

Beyond its Qing-dynasty architecture, this area hides family-run stalls slinging xiao long bao (soup dumplings) for $3 a steamer.

Pro Tips for Street Food Newbies

  • Cash is king: Many vendors don’t accept mobile payments.
  • Follow the queues: Long lines = fresh batches.
  • Spice wisely: Nanjing loves mild flavors, but chili paste is always on standby.

Nanjing’s streets are a buffet of bold flavors, and with this list, you’ll eat like royalty—without spending like one. Every bite tells a story, and at these prices, you can afford to taste them all.
