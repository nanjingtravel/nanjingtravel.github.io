Nanjing Woodblock Prints as Souvenirs

  • Home
  • Travel Blog
  • Blog Details

The hunt for the perfect souvenir is a quiet, persistent theme in any traveler's journey. It’s the search for an object that can do the impossible: to crystallize the essence of a place, its history, and the personal experience of being there, into a single, tangible form. In a world saturated with mass-produced keychains and factory-printed t-shirts, this search becomes even more poignant. For the discerning traveler to Nanjing, a city where the weight of history is as palpable as the humidity in the summer air, the answer is not found on a spinning rack, but in the fine, deliberate lines of a traditional art form: the Nanjing Woodblock Print.

This isn't just a picture to hang on a wall. It is a piece of the city's soul, inked and pressed onto paper, a narrative carved from wood and born from a tradition that is as enduring as the Ming Dynasty city wall itself.

More Than a Picture: The Story in the Grain

To understand why a woodblock print is such a remarkable souvenir, one must first move beyond seeing it as a simple image. It is a physical process, a story told in three acts: the carving, the inking, and the pressing.

The Language of the Carving Knife

An artist begins with a block of wood, typically a fine-grained hardwood like pear. The design is drawn directly onto the wood's surface. Then, with a set of specialized knives and gouges, the artist begins the subtractive process. Every line you see as white in the final print is where the artist has meticulously carved away the wood. The raised areas that remain will hold the ink. This is a language of negative space and positive form. The pressure of the hand, the depth of the cut, the texture left by the tool—all of these are embedded into the final work. When you hold a print, you are holding the ghost of that labor, the memory of the artist's hand forcing a image out of resistance.

The Alchemy of Ink and Paper

Once the block is carved, the artist carefully applies ink using a dauber or a brayer. The consistency of the ink, the evenness of the application—these are skills honed over years. Then, a sheet of paper, often a delicate yet strong Xuan paper (known as shuanzhi), is placed on the inked block. The back of the paper is rubbed methodically with a baren—a flat, disc-like pad. This is not a mechanical stamp; it’s a transfer of energy. The pressure varies, ensuring that the ink is picked up perfectly, often leaving a slight embossment, the bas-relief of the block itself, on the paper. This singular, hand-pressed nature means no two prints are absolutely identical. Your souvenir is unique, a one-of-a-kind moment captured from a process that can be repeated but never perfectly replicated.

Nanjing Through the Printer's Eye: Iconic Motifs for the Traveler

Nanjing woodblock prints are not generic Chinese scenes. They are deeply tied to the city's landscape and legacy. The artists draw from a well of local iconography, transforming the city's most famous sites into evocative, often poetic, visual narratives. For a tourist, this is the ultimate treasure: a beautiful representation of the very places they have just explored.

The Soul of the City: The Qinhuai River Lanterns

No motif is more quintessentially Nanjing than scenes of the Qinhuai River. The prints capture the area not with photographic realism, but with a dreamlike atmosphere. You see the elegant curves of the old-style boats (qinhuaichuan), the soft glow of lanterns reflecting on the dark water, and the silhouettes of the traditional architecture along its banks. The artists masterfully use the woodblock medium to create a sense of bustling life amidst serene waters. The lines for the water might be long and flowing, while the crowds on the shore are suggested with a myriad of tiny, energetic cuts. Owning such a print is like owning a piece of that magical evening stroll along the river, the laughter and the melodies from the boats forever suspended in ink.

A Monarch's Rest: The Ming Xiaoling Mausoleum

The solemn and majestic spirit of the Ming Xiaoling Mausoleum, a UNESCO World Heritage site, translates powerfully into the woodblock medium. Prints of this site often emphasize its grandeur and its dialogue with nature. The long, towering path of the Spirit Way, lined with stone animals and officials, is rendered with strong, vertical lines that convey a sense of awe and procession. The prints might focus on a single, dramatic detail—the immense, moss-covered bixi turtle stele, or the silent, imposing stone generals guarding the path. The texture of the wood grain itself can be used to suggest the weathering of centuries on the stone surfaces. It’s a souvenir that carries the quiet dignity and historical profundity of the place, far more resonant than a simple photograph.

The City's Shield: The Ancient City Wall

Nanjing's City Wall is not just a structure; it is the defining boundary of the old city, a ribbon of stone and history. Woodblock prints depicting the wall are studies in texture and scale. Artists use cross-hatching and varied line work to recreate the rough-hewn surface of the bricks. They often play with perspectives, showing the wall snaking over a hill, with the modern skyline of Nanjing peeking from behind its ancient battlements. This juxtaposition of old and new is a central theme in contemporary Nanjing, and these prints capture that dialogue perfectly. It’s a reminder of the city's resilience, a shield that has stood for over 600 years.

Beyond the Tourist Trail: The Modern Pulse of an Ancient Art

While traditional scenes remain popular, the woodblock print scene in Nanjing is not a museum piece frozen in time. Contemporary artists are actively using this ancient medium to comment on modern life, making the art form incredibly relevant for today's traveler.

Finding a Studio: An Unforgettable Cultural Experience

For a truly immersive experience, seeking out a local printmaking studio or workshop is a must. Places like the Nanjing University of the Arts or smaller, independent studios in the Lao Men Dong (Old Gate Area) often host exhibitions or short workshops. Participating in a one-day class is perhaps the most profound souvenir one can acquire—the memory of creating your own print. Under guidance, you can carve a simple design—perhaps your initials in a traditional seal script, or a stylized symbol of something you saw in Nanjing—and then experience the thrill of the first press, seeing your own creation come to life. This transforms you from a passive consumer into a participant in the tradition.

Contemporary Visions: Prints for the 21st Century

You might find prints that depict the sleek lines of the Zifeng Tower juxtaposed with a traditional courtyard, or scenes of the buzzing Confucius Temple area (Fuzimiao) rendered in a bold, modernist style. Some artists create whimsical prints of Nanjing's beloved salted duck, or maps of the city that are both artistic and functional. These contemporary pieces make for fantastic, conversation-starting souvenirs because they speak to the Nanjing of today—a city that honors its past while racing towards the future. They prove that the woodblock is not a relic, but a living, breathing form of artistic expression.

The Thoughtful Traveler's Guide to Acquiring a Print

Knowing where and what to look for is key to finding an authentic and meaningful woodblock print.

Seeking Authenticity: Where to Look

Avoid the generic souvenir shops that cluster around the biggest tourist sites. Instead, head to dedicated art galleries, the shops attached to artist studios, or cultural markets. The Nanjing Museum often has a high-quality gift shop with curated prints. Look for signs that indicate the artist is local. A genuine print will often have the artist's chop (a red seal) stamped in the corner, along with a pencil signature and edition number (e.g., 15/100). This not only authenticates the work but connects you directly to its creator.

Appreciating the Craft: What Makes a Quality Print

Don't be shy about asking questions. A reputable seller will be proud to explain the process. Look for the clarity and sharpness of the lines. Is the registration (the alignment of different color blocks, if it's a multi-color print) precise? Feel the paper—high-quality shuanzhi has a distinct, soft texture. Notice the depth of the ink. A good print will have a rich, velvety black that sits on the surface of the paper rather than bleeding through it. You are not just buying an image; you are investing in the quality of its creation.

In the end, a Nanjing woodblock print is more than a travel memento. It is a slice of the city's cultural heartbeat, a story of craftsmanship, history, and personal journey, all pressed onto a sheet of paper. It carries the scent of ink and the spirit of the ancient capital. It is a quiet, powerful artifact that doesn't just say "I was there," but whispers, "I understood, and I brought a piece of its soul back with me."
