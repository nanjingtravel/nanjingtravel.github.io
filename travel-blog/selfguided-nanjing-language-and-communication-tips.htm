Self-Guided Nanjing: Language and Communication Tips

  • Home
  • Travel Blog
  • Blog Details

Nanjing, a city where ancient city walls silently narrate tales of empires and modern skyscrapers pierce the sky, offers an intoxicating blend of history and hyper-modernity. For the independent traveler, navigating this former capital without a tour guide can seem daunting, especially when faced with the intricacies of the Chinese language. But fear not. This guide is your key to unlocking Nanjing confidently, turning potential communication hurdles into rewarding cultural exchanges. Your journey into the heart of Jiangsu province begins not just with a map, but with a few essential words and a willingness to connect.

Before You Go: Laying the Groundwork

A little preparation goes a long way in ensuring a smooth Nanjing adventure. While you're packing your bags, make some digital preparations as well.

Essential Digital Lifelines

Your smartphone will be your most valuable travel companion. Before departing, ensure you have these apps installed and ready.

  • WeChat (Weixin): This is not an option; it's a necessity. It's your WhatsApp, Facebook, payment wallet, and more, all rolled into one. Set up WeChat Pay if you can, as it's ubiquitous. While linking it to a foreign card can be tricky, having the app is crucial for scanning QR codes, communicating with hotel staff, and more.
  • A Robust VPN: Familiar services like Google (including Maps and Translate), Instagram, and WhatsApp are blocked in China. A reliable VPN is essential for accessing these services. Set it up before you arrive in China.
  • Pleco: This is the gold-standard Chinese-English dictionary app. Its optical character recognition (OCR) feature is a game-changer—simply point your camera at a menu or sign, and it will translate the characters for you. Download the offline dictionaries for when you're without data.
  • Didi: China's answer to Uber. The app has an English interface and is perfect for hailing taxis or private cars. It automatically handles the address communication with the driver, eliminating a major point of confusion.
  • Baidu Maps or Amap: While Google Maps can be unreliable due to the blocking, these local alternatives are accurate and detailed, even if their English interfaces can be clunky. They are excellent for navigating the superb Nanjing Metro system.

Language Bootcamp: The 10-Phrase Survival Kit

You don't need fluency, but mastering a few key phrases will transform your experience. Practice the pronunciation before you go.

  • Hello: Nǐ hǎo (Nee how)
  • Thank you: Xièxie (Shyeh-shyeh)
  • You're welcome: Bù kèqì (Boo kuh-chee)
  • Sorry/Excuse me: Duìbuqǐ (Dway-boo-chee) - Use this to get someone's attention.
  • How much?: Duōshao qián? (Dwaw-shhow chyen?)
  • I don't understand: Wǒ tīng bù dǒng (Woh ting boo dong)
  • This one: Zhège (Jay-guh) - Incredibly useful when pointing at food or items.
  • Check, please: Mǎidān (My-dan)
  • No, thank you: Bù yào, xièxie (Boo yow, shyeh-shyeh)
  • Beautiful: Piàoliang (Peeow-lyang) - Perfect for complimenting the scenery at places like the Sun Yat-sen Mausoleum.

On the Ground: Navigating Nanjing's Hotspots

Nanjing's attractions are spread out, but the city's efficient metro system makes them accessible. Here’s how to communicate at key locations.

Conquering the Confucius Temple (Fuzimiao) Qinhuai Scenic Area

This bustling area is a sensory delight, with ancient architecture, street food, and boat rides on the Qinhuai River. Communication here is fast-paced.

  • Street Food Adventures: Don't be shy. Point at what looks good and say, "Zhège, yí gè" (This one, one). For Nanjing's famous salted duck, you could try "Yào yāzi" (I want duck). Vendors are used to tourists and will often show you the price on a calculator.
  • Bargaining in the Markets: Bargaining is expected in the small souvenir shops. The vendor might say "Yībǎi kuài" (100 kuai/RMB). You can counter with "Tài guì le! Wǔshí kuài?" (Too expensive! Fifty kuai?). Be polite and smile—it's a game, not a argument.
  • Asking for Directions: To find the river for a boat ride, you can ask, "Qǐngwèn, chuán zài nǎlǐ?" (Excuse me, where is the boat?). Use your map app to point if words fail.

Scaling the Sun Yat-sen Mausoleum (Zhongshan Ling)

The sheer scale and grandeur of this monument can leave you speechless. The communication needs here are simpler but still important.

  • Tickets and Transport: At the entrance, you'll see signs for "piào" (ticket). The ticket office is "shòupiào chù." To get to the foot of the mausoleum from the metro station, you'll need to take the tourist shuttle. Show the driver your destination on your phone map or simply say "Zhōngshān Líng."
  • Respectful Conduct: This is a place of great reverence. Keep your voice down. You'll hear and see the word "sù jìng" (silence and respect). Following this unspoken rule is a form of universal communication.

Walking the Nanjing City Wall & the Presidential Palace

These two sites offer a tangible connection to Nanjing's Ming dynasty past and its republican era.

  • City Wall Access: The wall has multiple access points. You might need to ask, "Nǎlǐ kěyǐ shàng chéngqiáng?" (Where can I go up on the city wall?). The section near the Jiming Temple offers stunning views.
  • Historical Context at the Presidential Palace: This complex is a history lesson in itself. While plaques are in English and Chinese, using your Pleco app to translate specific exhibits can provide deeper insight. The phrase "hěn yǒu yìsi" (very interesting) is a great way to express your appreciation to a staff member.

Mastering Daily Interactions

Beyond the tourist sites, it's the day-to-day interactions that define your trip.

The Art of Payment: Cash, QR Codes, and Cards

China is a largely cashless society, but cash is still king for foreign tourists.

  • Cash is King (For You): Always carry a supply of smaller denomination bills (10, 20, 50 RMB). For taxis, small shops, and street vendors, cash is your primary payment method.
  • The QR Code World: You will see QR codes for everything from ordering in a restaurant to paying for a museum audio guide. While WeChat Pay and Alipay are challenging for short-term visitors to set up for payments, you can still scan codes to access menus or information. If a vendor insists on QR payment, just smile, show your cash, and say "Xiànjīn" (cash).
  • Tipping: Tipping is not practiced in China. It can even be considered rude. A sincere "Xièxie" is all that's needed.

Dining Decoded: From Noodle Shops to Fine Restaurants

Eating in Nanjing is a highlight. Don't let an untranslated menu stop you.

  • The Point-and-Smile Method: Universally effective. Walk over to a table where something looks delicious, point, and ask "Zhège?"
  • Using Translation Apps: This is where Pleco's camera function shines. Scan the menu for recognizable characters like "niúròu" (beef), "jī" (chicken), "miàn" (noodles), or "fàn" (rice).
  • Key Food Vocabulary:
    • Menu: Càidān (Tsai-dan)
    • Water: Shuǐ (Shway)
    • Beer: Píjiǔ (Pee-jeeoh)
    • Vegetables: Shūcài (Shoo-tsai)
    • Not spicy: Bù là (Boo lah)
    • Delicious: Hǎo chī (How chir)

Transportation Talk: Metro, Taxis, and Didi

Nanjing's metro is clean, efficient, and easy to use with signs in Pinyin.

  • Metro: Buy a single-journey token from the automated machines, which have an English language option. Alternatively, purchase a rechargeable transportation card ("Jiāotōng kǎ") from a ticket counter for convenience.
  • Taxis: Have your destination written in Chinese characters on your phone or a piece of paper. This is the single most effective tip for taking taxis. Saying an address in English will almost never work.
  • Didi: As mentioned, this app is your best friend. It handles the address, the route, and the payment (if you set it up with a card), making the entire process seamless.

The true language of Nanjing travel is a smile, a patient attitude, and a sense of adventure. Every miscommunication is a story, and every successful interaction, whether through a perfectly pronounced "xièxie" or a shared laugh over a pointing game, is a genuine connection with this profound and captivating city. Your self-guided journey will be all the richer for it.
