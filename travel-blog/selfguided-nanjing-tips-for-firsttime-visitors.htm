Self-Guided Nanjing: Tips for First-Time Visitors

  • Home
  • Travel Blog
  • Blog Details

Nanjing, the capital of China’s Jiangsu province, sits on the Yangtze River and boasts a rich history as the former capital of several Chinese dynasties. For first-time visitors, the city offers a compelling mix of ancient heritage and modern energy. A self-guided tour allows you to explore at your own pace, diving deep into the stories that intrigue you most. This guide will help you navigate the city’s highlights, from majestic historical sites to vibrant local markets, ensuring an unforgettable and smooth journey.

Crafting Your Perfect Nanjing Itinerary

A well-planned itinerary is your best friend in Nanjing. The city is sprawling, and its attractions are spread out, but with a little strategy, you can cover a lot of ground without feeling rushed.

A Three-Day Blueprint for History and Culture

If you have three full days, this itinerary balances profound historical sites with leisurely exploration.

Day 1: The Weight of History Start your journey with the city's most poignant site, the Nanjing Massacre Memorial Hall. This is a solemn and essential visit, offering a powerful and moving tribute to the victims of the 1937 atrocity. Allocate at least three hours here to fully absorb the exhibits. The atmosphere is heavy, so plan for a quiet lunch afterwards.

In the afternoon, head to the Presidential Palace. This massive complex tells the story of China's modern history, having served as the headquarters for the Taiping Heavenly Kingdom, the Qing government, and finally, the Republic of China. You can easily spend three hours wandering through the beautiful gardens and historic offices.

Day 2: Imperial Grandeur and City Walls Dedicate your second morning to the Zhongshan Mountain Scenic Area. This vast park is home to the Sun Yat-sen Mausoleum, an architectural masterpiece honoring the founding father of modern China. Climb the 392 steps for a breathtaking view and a sense of accomplishment. Nearby, explore the Ming Xiaoling Mausoleum, the magnificent tomb of the Hongwu Emperor, the founder of the Ming Dynasty. The Spirit Way, lined with stone animals and statues, is a highlight.

In the late afternoon, make your way to the Jiming Temple. As the oldest and one of the most revered Buddhist temples in Nanjing, it offers a serene escape. Climb the pagoda for a fantastic panoramic view of the city and Xuanwu Lake, especially beautiful during sunset.

Day 3: Ancient Streets and Cityscapes Begin your final day with a stroll along the Qinhuai River. This area was the center of entertainment and commerce in ancient times. Visit the Confucius Temple (Fuzimiao), a bustling area with traditional architecture, street food, and souvenir shops. Consider a boat ride on the river for a different perspective.

For your last major stop, head to a preserved section of the Nanjing City Wall. The best segment to walk is near the Jiming Temple or the Zhonghua Gate (Zhonghuamen), the largest ancient city gate in the world. Walking atop this 600-year-old fortification gives you a unique vantage point of the old and new city.

In the evening, explore the lively Confucius Temple area once more, now illuminated by lanterns, and indulge in a final feast of local delicacies.

Off-the-Beaten-Path Gems

If you have more time or want to escape the crowds, consider these additions: * Xuanwu Lake: A massive and beautiful lake park perfect for a relaxing bike ride or a leisurely walk. * Nanjing Museum: One of China's premier museums, with an impressive collection of artifacts, including a stunning jade burial suit. * Qixia Mountain: A scenic area famous for its autumn foliage and the Qixia Temple, a significant Buddhist site.

Navigating the City Like a Pro

Getting around Nanjing is straightforward thanks to its excellent public transportation system.

Mastering the Metro

The Nanjing Metro is clean, efficient, and well-signposted in English. It is by far the best way to travel between major districts and attractions. Purchase a rechargeable IC card (Jinlingtong) at any metro station if you plan on using public transport frequently; it offers a slight discount on fares and can also be used on buses and taxis. Alternatively, single-journey tokens are easy to buy from automated machines.

Buses, Taxis, and Ride-Hailing

Buses cover every corner of the city but can be challenging for non-Mandarin speakers due to less English signage. However, for shorter, well-researched routes, they are very cheap.

Taxis are plentiful and relatively inexpensive. Always ensure the driver uses the meter. For even more convenience, use ride-hailing apps like Didi Chuxing, which has an English interface and eliminates the language barrier when inputting your destination.

The Joy of Walking and Biking

Many of Nanjing's attractions, especially in the Qinhuai River area, are best explored on foot. The city is also very bike-friendly, with numerous bike-sharing services (like Meituan Bike and Hello Bike) available. Download the respective apps, scan a QR code, and you're off—a fantastic and eco-friendly way to explore neighborhoods and lakeside paths.

Savoring the Flavors of Nanjing

Nanjing's cuisine is part of the Jiangsu culinary tradition, known for its freshness and subtle, slightly sweet flavors.

Must-Try Local Dishes

  • Nanjing Salted Duck (Yanshui Ya): This is the city's most famous dish. Unlike the roasted Peking duck, Nanjing's version is brined and stewed, resulting in tender, flavorful meat with a smooth, non-greasy skin.
  • Duck Blood and Vermicelli Soup (Yaxue Fensi Tang): A beloved street food and comfort food. The soup features chunks of congealed duck blood, vermicelli noodles, and various offal in a savory broth.
  • Soup Dumplings (Tangbao): While Shanghai is more famous for them, Nanjing offers excellent versions. These are large dumplings filled with hot, flavorful soup and minced meat.
  • Pan-Fried Beef Dumplings (Guo tie): Crispy on the bottom and juicy on the inside, these are a perfect snack.
  • Sweet and Sour Mandarin Fish (Squirrel Fish): A visually stunning and delicious dish where a whole fish is scored, fried until crispy, and served with a rich sweet and sour sauce.

Where to Eat

  • Confucius Temple (Fuzimiao) Area: This is the epicenter for tourists and offers a huge variety of snacks and restaurants in a traditional setting. It's a great place to try multiple small bites.
  • Zifeng Tower / Xinjiekou Area: As the modern downtown, this area is filled with shopping malls that have extensive food courts and higher-end restaurants catering to all tastes and budgets.
  • Local Food Streets: Explore streets like Shiziqiao or Hunan Road for more authentic, local eateries where you can see what the residents are eating.

Essential Practical Tips for a Smooth Trip

A little preparation goes a long way in ensuring a hassle-free adventure.

Money and Communication

The currency is the Chinese Yuan (CNY or RMB). Credit cards are widely accepted in hotels and large stores, but for street food, small shops, and some taxis, you will need cash. ATMs are readily available.

While you can manage with English at major hotels and tourist sites, it's much less common elsewhere. Learning a few basic Mandarin phrases like "Nǐ hǎo" (Hello), "Xièxie" (Thank you), and "Duōshǎo qián?" (How much?) will be appreciated. Having your hotel's address written in Chinese characters is also very helpful for taxi drivers. A reliable VPN is essential for accessing Google services, social media, and other websites restricted behind the Great Firewall.

The Best Time to Visit

Nanjing has a humid subtropical climate with four distinct seasons. * Spring (March-May) and Autumn (September-November) are the best times to visit. The weather is mild and pleasant, perfect for sightseeing. Autumn is particularly beautiful with fall foliage, especially at Qixia Mountain. * Summer (June-August) is hot, humid, and rainy. Be prepared for high temperatures and occasional downpours. * Winter (December-February) is cold and damp, with occasional snow. While less crowded, the weather can be challenging for extensive outdoor exploration.

Cultural Etiquette and Staying Safe

Nanjing is a very safe city with a low crime rate. Standard precautions against pickpocketing in crowded areas like markets and metro stations are advisable.

When visiting temples and mausoleums, dress modestly. Temples are active places of worship, so maintain a respectful demeanor. When bargaining in markets, do so with a smile and be prepared to walk away if the price isn't right. Nanjing is a city deeply proud of its history, and showing respect for its past, particularly at solemn sites like the Massacre Memorial, is of utmost importance.
