The Colors of Nanjing: Palette of Its Historic Facades

  • Home
  • Travel Blog
  • Blog Details

Nanjing, a city where history whispers through every brick and tile, wears its past like a vibrant tapestry. From the deep reds of ancient temples to the muted grays of wartime relics, its facades tell stories of dynasties, revolutions, and rebirth. For travelers, these colors aren’t just visual delights—they’re gateways to understanding the soul of a city that has been China’s capital multiple times over.

The Crimson Legacy: Imperial Nanjing

The Vermilion Gates of the Ming Xiaoling Mausoleum

No color symbolizes Nanjing’s imperial past more vividly than vermilion. The Ming Xiaoling Mausoleum, resting place of the Hongwu Emperor, greets visitors with towering crimson gates that seem to bleed into the surrounding greenery. This shade wasn’t chosen at random—it represented power, prosperity, and the mandate of heaven.

Tourists often linger here, not just for photos but to trace the intricate carvings of dragons and phoenixes, their details still sharp despite six centuries of weathering. Nearby, the Sacred Way’s stone statues stand guard under a canopy of gingko trees, their golden leaves in autumn creating a striking contrast against the red walls.

The Forbidden City’s Echo: Nanjing’s Purple Gold Mountain

While Beijing’s Forbidden City hogs the spotlight, Nanjing’s own imperial remnants—like the ruins of the Ming Palace—offer a quieter, more introspective journey. The Purple Gold Mountain (Zijin Shan) frames these relics in hues of deep purple at dawn, a phenomenon locals say inspired its name. Hiking trails here are dotted with pavilions painted in traditional zhū hóng (vermilion), their eaves curling toward the sky like brushstrokes on a classical painting.

The Gray Hues: Nanjing’s Modern Struggles

The Nanjing Massacre Memorial Hall

Gray dominates the Memorial Hall, a sobering tribute to the victims of 1937. The architecture—deliberately stark—uses concrete and steel to evoke weight and sorrow. Visitors move through exhibits in near silence, the only splashes of color coming from origami cranes left by mourners. This isn’t just a stop on a tourist itinerary; it’s a pilgrimage for those seeking to grasp the city’s resilience.

The Brick Red of Republic-Era Architecture

Along Zhongshan Road, the brick red facades of early 20th-century buildings whisper of Nanjing’s brief stint as China’s capital under the Republic. These structures blend Western art deco with Chinese motifs—a visual metaphor for the era’s cultural collisions. The Presidential Palace, now a museum, is a standout, its red walls and white columns reflecting Sun Yat-sen’s vision of a modern China.

The Emerald and Jade: Nature’s Brushstrokes

Xuanwu Lake’s Seasonal Palette

Nanjing’s "lungs," Xuanwu Lake, shifts colors with the seasons: cherry blossoms pink in spring, lotus leaves emerald in summer, maple trees fiery in autumn. The lake’s causeways—lined with qīng (blue-green) pavilions—are perfect for slow strolls. Rent a paddleboat at sunset, and you’ll see the city skyline turn gold against the water.

The Green Canopy of City Walls

The Ming-era city walls, some of the best-preserved in China, are cloaked in ivy and moss. Climbing the steps near Jiming Temple rewards you with a view of mò lǜ (ink-green) rooftops sprawling toward the Yangtze. Cyclists love the wall-top path, especially in April when wisteria drips purple from the ramparts.

The Golden Glow: Nanjing’s Culinary Colors

Duck Blood and Vermicelli Soup: A Dark Red Delicacy

Food in Nanjing is as colorful as its architecture. The infamous yā xiě fěnsī tāng (duck blood and vermicelli soup) shocks first-timers with its deep red broth, but one sip reveals a rich, peppery warmth. Street vendors serve it in stainless steel bowls, garnished with cilantro—a green flourish against the crimson.

Sweet Osmanthus Gold

Autumn brings guìhuā (osmanthus) blooms, and the city sweetens everything from rice cakes to tea with their fragrance. The best place to sample this? The Confucius Temple area, where stalls sell guìhuā táng (osmanthus candy) wrapped in gold foil, a nod to Nanjing’s history as a hub of imperial examinations.

The Neon Nights: Modern Nanjing’s Dazzle

The LED Symphony of Confucius Temple

By night, the Qinhuai River transforms into a neon dreamscape. The Confucius Temple district lights up in electric blues and pinks, its reflection shimmering on the water. Tourists flock here for river cruises, but the real magic is in the back alleys, where calligraphers sell ink paintings under strings of dēnglóng (lanterns).

1912 District: Jazz and Jade Cocktails

For a contemporary twist, the 1912 bar district mixes colonial-era buildings with glowing signage. Rooftop bars like The Nest serve cocktails in jade-colored glasses—a modern homage to Nanjing’s love of green. Live jazz floats through the air, blending with the clink of glasses.

The Unseen Colors: Hidden Corners

Porcelain Tower’s Lost Glaze

Though the Porcelain Tower of Bao’en Temple is long gone (a replica now stands in its place), historians still obsess over its original lǐuli (glazed) tiles—said to have shimmered in rainbow hues. The nearby museum displays fragments, their cobalt blues and celadons hinting at the tower’s former glory.

Laomendong’s Pastel Street Art

In the artsy Laomendong district, pastel murals brighten crumbling lǎo fángzi (old houses). Instagrammers adore the candy-colored doorways, but the area’s true charm lies in its artisan workshops—think indigo-dyed fabrics and hand-painted kites in sunflower yellow.

Nanjing’s colors aren’t just seen; they’re felt. They linger in the scent of osmanthus after rain, in the echo of footsteps on ancient stone, and in the way sunset gilds the Yangtze’s waves. To travel here is to step into a living painting—one where every hue has a history.
