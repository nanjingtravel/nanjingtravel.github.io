The Most Unique Foods to Try at Nanjing Night Markets

  • Home
  • Travel Blog
  • Blog Details

Nanjing, the ancient capital of China, is not only rich in history but also a paradise for food lovers. The city’s night markets are a sensory overload—bustling with energy, vibrant colors, and mouthwatering aromas. If you’re a traveler looking to dive into Nanjing’s culinary scene, here’s a guide to the most unique foods you must try at its legendary night markets.

The Must-Try Street Food Staples

Saltwater Duck (Yánshuǐ Yā)

Nanjing’s most famous dish, Saltwater Duck, is a must-try. Unlike Peking Duck, this version is brined and simmered in a fragrant broth, resulting in tender, juicy meat with a subtle salty-savory flavor. Vendors at night markets often serve it sliced thin, perfect for grabbing on the go.

Stinky Tofu (Chòu Dòufu)

Love it or hate it, Stinky Tofu is an iconic street food. Fermented for days, this pungent tofu is deep-fried until crispy and served with spicy sauce. The strong aroma might be intimidating, but the crispy exterior and creamy interior make it a favorite among locals.

Tangbao (Soup Dumplings)

While Shanghai has its xiaolongbao, Nanjing’s tangbao are a unique twist. These oversized soup dumplings are filled with rich, hot broth and minced pork. The trick is to take a small bite first to sip the soup before devouring the rest.

Unusual Delicacies You Won’t Find Everywhere

Duck Blood and Vermicelli Soup (Yāxuè Fěnsī Tāng)

This might sound bizarre, but it’s a beloved Nanjing specialty. Silky duck blood cubes float in a savory broth with chewy vermicelli, tofu puffs, and sometimes duck offal. The texture is smooth, and the flavor is deeply umami.

Grilled Squid with Spicy Sauce

Nanjing’s night markets take seafood to the next level. Fresh squid is grilled over open flames, basted with a sweet-spicy glaze, and served on skewers. The smoky aroma and chewy texture make it irresistible.

Fried Cicadas (Zhīliǎo)

For the adventurous eater, fried cicadas are a crunchy, protein-packed snack. Seasoned with chili and cumin, they taste nutty and slightly earthy. Don’t knock it till you try it!

Sweet Treats to Satisfy Your Cravings

Osmanthus Glutinous Rice Cake (Guìhuā Gāo)

A fragrant dessert made with sticky rice and sweet osmanthus flowers, this cake is soft, slightly chewy, and subtly floral. It’s often steamed and served warm, making it a comforting end to a night of feasting.

Sweet Fermented Rice Soup (Jiǔniàng Yuánzi)

This warm, sweet soup features glutinous rice balls floating in a fermented rice broth. The slight tanginess of the fermented rice balances the sweetness, creating a unique flavor profile.

Candied Hawthorns (Bīngtánghúlu)

Skewered hawthorn berries coated in hardened sugar syrup—this classic Chinese street snack is both tart and sweet. The crisp sugar shell contrasts beautifully with the soft, tangy fruit inside.

Drinks to Wash It All Down

Sugarcane Juice (Gānzhè Zhī)

Freshly pressed sugarcane juice is a refreshing, naturally sweet drink that pairs perfectly with spicy street food. Vendors often serve it over ice for an extra cooling effect.

Sour Plum Drink (Sūanméi Tāng)

A traditional Chinese beverage made from smoked plums, rock sugar, and herbs, this drink is both sweet and tangy. It’s also believed to aid digestion—perfect after a heavy meal.

Bubble Tea (Bōbà Nǎichá)

While not unique to Nanjing, the city’s night markets offer creative twists on bubble tea, including flavors like osmanthus or taro. The chewy tapioca pearls make it a fun, textural experience.

Pro Tips for Navigating Nanjing Night Markets

  • Go early or late—markets are busiest around 8-10 PM.
  • Bring cash—many vendors don’t accept digital payments.
  • Follow the locals—the longest lines usually lead to the best food.
  • Try small portions—this way, you can sample more dishes without filling up too quickly.

Nanjing’s night markets are a treasure trove of flavors, from the familiar to the downright daring. Whether you’re a foodie or just curious, these unique dishes will make your visit unforgettable.
